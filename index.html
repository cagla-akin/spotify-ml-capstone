Executive Summary

The Surprise Finding: Sound Doesn't Drive Popularity

This project builds a machine learning model to predict the popularity of Spotify tracks, scored 0–100 by Spotify's algorithm. Using a dataset of 169,909 tracks spanning 1921–2020, the goal was to identify what actually drives streaming popularity — and what does not.

Five models were trained and evaluated: Dummy Regressor, Linear Regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost (default and tuned). The best-performing model is XGBoost with default parameters, achieving RMSE = 9.65 and R² = 0.72 on a held-out test set of 28,511 tracks. Hyperparameter tuning via RandomizedSearchCV (50 iterations, 3-fold CV) produced no meaningful improvement — tuned RMSE = 9.72, confirming that default parameters are near-optimal for this dataset.

The most important finding is not the model's accuracy but what it reveals about popularity itself: release year (r = 0.82) and artist average popularity (r = 0.72) together account for 97% of the model's predictive weight. All 13 audio features — danceability, energy, loudness, acousticness, valence, tempo, and more — contribute less than 3% combined. A great-sounding song from an unknown artist will almost always underperform a mediocre track from an established star.

For Spotify, which paid approximately $9B in royalties in 2023 (~70% of total revenue), this finding has direct commercial implications: royalty rates set against audio quality are set against the wrong signal. The dominant drivers of popularity — artist reputation and release timing — are knowable before a contract is signed.

Dataset & Data Cleaning

169,909 Tracks, Zero Missing Values, One Major Decision

The dataset was sourced from Kaggle and contains tracks pulled from the Spotify Web API, covering releases from 1921 to 2020. Each of the 169,909 rows represents a unique Spotify track ID and contains 19 columns: nine continuous audio features (acousticness, danceability, energy, instrumentalness, liveness, loudness, speechiness, tempo, valence), four categorical columns (artists, id, name, release_date), the binary explicit flag, musical key, mode, duration, year, and the target variable popularity.

A full quality check confirmed zero missing values across all 169,909 rows. No imputation was required. The only cleaning decision of consequence was handling zero-popularity tracks.

Tracks removed from dataset

27,357

16.1% of raw tracks carry a popularity score of exactly 0, indicating Spotify has insufficient streaming data — not that the song was heard and disliked. Including these would add noise to the target without representing genuine listening behaviour. All tracks with popularity ≥ 1 were retained: 142,552 active tracks.

All versions are retained because each is a distinct Spotify product with its own streaming history, and year is included as a feature to allow the model to distinguish between them.

1

Year dominates: r = 0.82

Release year is the strongest raw predictor — 2x stronger than the next-best audio feature (loudness, r = 0.41). This is structural: Spotify's popularity score reflects recent streaming activity, so newer tracks systematically score higher. Decade averages climb from 7 (1920s) to 66 (2020s). This is recency bias, not song quality.

2

Audio features are weak individually

Among the 13 raw audio features, acousticness (r = -0.47) and loudness (r = 0.41) show the strongest correlations. All others fall below |r| = 0.40. Tempo, liveness, speechiness, valence, and key are all below |r| = 0.09. Once year and artist context are added to the model, audio contributes less than 3% of predictive weight.

3

Explicit tracks average 18.5 points higher

12,357 explicit tracks (8.7% of the dataset) average popularity 54.5. The 130,195 clean tracks average 36.0. This reflects genre concentration: explicit content is disproportionately associated with hip-hop and R&B, which dominate recent streaming charts rather than being an independent quality signal.

4

Popularity is right-skewed, mean = 37.6

After removing zero-popularity tracks, the distribution has mean = 37.6, median = 38.0, std = 18.1. Fewer tracks achieve high popularity (80–100) than mid-range (20–60). No transformation was applied: tree-based models are robust to skewed targets.

5

Re-releases vary by up to 86 popularity points

The same song released in different years can score dramatically differently. Average spread across re-releases is 13.6 points; maximum is 86 points. This confirms year as a meaningful feature beyond just recency bias — it distinguishes real versions of the same track.

6

Top artists outperform bottom artists by 77 points

Among 2,705 artists with 10 or more tracks, Billie Eilish (78.8), Harry Styles (77.0), and Lewis Capaldi (76.9) rank highest. Bottom-ranked artists (primarily pre-1950s classical) average below 1.5. This 77-point spread motivates artist_avg_popularity as an engineered feature.

Feature Engineering & Pre-Processing

One Engineered Feature Drives Almost All Model Performance

Feature engineering was performed on the training set only and applied to the test set to prevent data leakage. The split (80/20, random_state=42) was performed before any transformation.

The most impactful step was engineering artist_avg_popularity: the mean popularity of each artist's top-3 tracks, computed from training data only. Unseen artists receive a fallback value of 44.09 (training mean). This single feature achieves r = 0.72 with the target and is computable from Spotify's API before a contract is signed, making it the most actionable pre-contract signal in the entire model.

Step 01

Drop identity columns

Remove id, name, release_date, decade — these do not generalise and would leak information.

Step 02 ✦

Engineer artist_avg_popularity

Mean of artist's top-3 track popularities, from train set only. Fallback = 44.09 for unseen artists. Achieves r = 0.72.

Step 03

One-hot encode key

Musical key (0–11) encoded to 11 binary columns using pd.get_dummies (drop_first=True to avoid multicollinearity).

Step 04

StandardScaler for LR only

Numeric features scaled before Linear Regression only. Fit on X_train, applied to X_test. Tree models receive unscaled inputs.

Final feature set: 25 columns. Numeric: acousticness, danceability, duration_ms, energy, explicit, instrumentalness, liveness, loudness, mode, speechiness, tempo, valence, year, artist_avg_popularity (14 features). One-hot encoded key: key_1 through key_11 (11 features). LabelEncoder was not used. StandardScaler was not applied to OHE columns.

Business Implications

What This Means for Spotify's Royalty Risk

Spotify paid approximately $9B in royalties in 2023, representing roughly 70% of total revenue. Royalty rates are negotiated before release against unknown future performance. The model's findings identify three concrete implications for how those rates should be assessed.

1

Low predicted popularity + premium rate = quantifiable financial risk

Artist average popularity is computable from Spotify data before a contract is signed and achieves r = 0.72 with actual track popularity. A model score below 30 for an artist receiving a premium royalty rate identifies a systematic overpayment risk — the track is structurally unlikely to generate the streams needed to justify the rate. This makes artist_avg_popularity the most actionable pre-contract screening metric available.

2

Marketing is the unmodelled driver that warrants further investigation

The model explains 72% of popularity variance using only historical data. The remaining 28% — the part the model cannot capture — likely includes playlist placements, editorial picks, social media campaigns, and sync licensing. These marketing signals are not in the dataset but are the mechanisms through which popularity is actively manufactured rather than passively observed. Quantifying their marginal contribution would materially improve royalty risk modelling.

3

Spotify's editorial activity creates leverage to promote lower-premium artists

If Spotify's own playlist curation and editorial activity are a primary driver of popularity (captured in the unexplained 28%), then Spotify has direct influence over which artists become popular. This creates a structural opportunity: by directing editorial support to artists on lower royalty rates, Spotify can improve streaming revenue per payout dollar. The platform is not a passive marketplace — it is an active participant in the popularity it then pays for.

Limitations

What the Model Cannot Tell You

R² = 0.72 is a meaningful result, but it is important to be clear about what the remaining 28% represents and where the model's boundaries lie.

01

Static 2021 snapshot: popularity decay is unmodelled

Popularity scores decay as streaming attention shifts to newer releases. The model uses scores captured at a single point in time. It systematically underestimates tracks that gained streams after 2021 and overestimates older catalogue whose rates have since declined.

02

Year correlation is structural, not causal

The r = 0.82 correlation between year and popularity reflects Spotify's recency-weighted scoring algorithm, not evidence that newer music is inherently better. A song from 2015 is not more popular than one from 1980 because it is higher quality — it is more popular because Spotify's algorithm prioritises recent streaming counts.

03

Spotify-only measure: cross-platform behaviour not captured

A track viral on TikTok, YouTube, or Apple Music may show low Spotify popularity in the training data. The model cannot generalise to overall commercial success — only to Spotify-specific streaming at one point in time.

04

No genre labels: audio proxies are incomplete

The dataset contains no genre column. Audio features act as imperfect genre proxies, which is why the explicit flag carries disproportionate weight — it is the closest available signal for hip-hop and R&B concentration. A genre label would likely absorb most of this effect cleanly.
